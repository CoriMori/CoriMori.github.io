About Me

I first got into video games when I was about 5 years old and from that point on I was hooked. I would often spend time drawing level layouts and coming up with game ideas for fun. When I was 12 I discovered programming and realized that I could start taking my ideas and making them a reality. Unfortunately I come from a very small town where education in technology was pretty far behind, so my programming experience was limited to HTML and CSS for the duration of high school. Dead set on joining the games industry, I enrolled in the Rochester Institute of Technology where I completed my Bachelors of Science in Game Design and Development and my minor in Psychology. For most of college I was unsure what I wanted to specialize in, so I tried a bit of everything. It wasn't until my senior year where programming really started to click for me, and it was at that point that I decided to focus on gameplay programming.

For the past three years I have worked as a Game Developer at a small studio where I was responsible for making small scale educational games from start to finish. I was often the only developer on these projects and this experience has allowed me to learn how to work quickly and efficiently while under pressure due to tight timelines. When I wasn't working on projects, I was working on improving the engine, workflow, and various Research and Development Projects. While I love programming, I am also very passionate about level and game design. In my previous position as a Game Developer I didn't get to work on level design or game design very much, but have kept those skills fresh though some of my personal projects.

If I'm not playing video games or working on Wizard Wars, you can probably find me going on a walk or a hike with my fianc√©e. If you're really lucky you might even catch us walking with our two cats; Delli and Benji. In a nutshell, I'm just a person who really likes games and wants to put as much fun into the world as possible.

Wizard Wars

Wizard Wars is a multiplayer 3rd person Prop Hunt game. This game was originally a way for me to learn Unreal Engine 5, but it has since become a very large focus of my free time. Wizard Wars operates using a client-server model via Steam Advanced Sessions to allow players to host and join servers with their friends. Once in the lobby, player can choose to between being the Hunters and being the Hiders. Hiders and Hunters have access to different spells to aid them in their hiding and seeking efforts in various ways.

As the sole designer and developer of Wizard Wars, I have faced my fair share of challenges. On the dev side, I started with limited knowledge of C++ and Unreal Engine and no knowledge of Unreal's Blueprint system. I still have a lot to learn, but I can now say that I am very confident in my abilities across the board when it comes to C++, Unreal Engine 5, and Unreal's Blueprint system.

On the design side, the biggest challenge I have faced is with balance. In other prop hunt games, I have always had an issue with how difficult it can be for the Hunters to win. In many games, Hiders are allowed to sit as the same prop the entire game with very little being done to allow the Hunters to find them. Newer games have tried to combat this with sound cues or by not allowing players to choose their prop and forcing the change at specific times. I decided to go a slightly different route. I added a duration to the spell that allows Hiders to become props. This forces players to change their hiding spots frequently or at least change props frequently as recasting the spell resets the duration. I also added a mana system for both Hunters and Hiders with a single refill point on the map. So when Hiders run low on mana, they have to move to a fairly dangerous spot to be able to keep hiding. By forcing interactions between Hunters and Hiders, it gives Hunters a slightly better chance at winning and makes things more interesting for the Hiders.

Quiet Thoughts

Quiet Thoughts is a 2D platformer game designed to be very challenging to players. As the level designer for this project, I was tasked with designing all the platforming areas. In each area I created multiple pathways through for players to take and chose spawn locations for various enemies and obstacles. I also assisted in playtesting the game prior to release.

One of the biggest challenges I faced with this project was balance. It is very difficult to design levels well when the difficulty is meant to be almost rage inducing. I spent a lot of time trying to balance the difficulty so the level was still possible, but not so easy that it didn't feel rewarding to get to the end. Quiet Thoughts is free to play here.

Blaze & the Monster Machines: Sports Day

Blaze and the Monster Machines: Sports Day is a suite of 5 sports themed minigames; Baseball, Basketball, Soccer, Hockey, and Pickleball. Players race against time to earn the most amount of points in each game and see how they stack up against their favorite Monster Machines. This game also features a co-play system, where 2 players can face off against each other by passing the device back and forth between rounds. As the developer on the this project, I was responsible for designing and implementing the mechanics, implementing art, animation VO, SFX, VFX, and music for each game.

Out of all of the minigames, Hockey was the most challenging to implement. In the hockey minigame, you can bounce the puck off of some angled bumpers. Translating something that is very 3D into a 2D space was something that took a good bit of time to figure out. To add to the challenge, there was also a goalie that could block your shots and then later a middle bumper. All of these needed to have the puck bounce off of them as well. The final solution involved around 6 raycasts being drawn to create a path for the puck to follow and if a hit was detected it would reflect off of the object it hit.

Bubble Guppies: Play House

Bubble Guppies: Play House is a sandbox game set in the Bubble Guppies universe. Players can explore 4 different rooms of a house, interact with their favorite guppies, and decorate the rooms however they want. Players complete literacy minigames to earn new items for rooms and even clothes and hats for the guppies. As the developer on the this project, I was responsible for designing and implementing the mechanics, implementing the object interactions, art, animation VO, SFX, VFX, and music. I was also heavily involved in the process of designing the different interactions between objects and characters.

This project was a challenge in general. Sandbox games are already pretty difficulty to create, but when you combine that with a small team and a short timeline it becomes even more difficult. Out of all of the challenges faced during this project, the most difficult was probably screen scrolling. Some rooms are 2 screens wide, so you would have to swipe to see the other half. The actual scrolling bit was fine, but there were already a lot touch and drag interactions happening on the characters and objects. It was very difficult to manage all of those on top of a full screen drag feature without things getting caught up.

Rubble & Crew: Ruff, Ruff, Repairs

Rubble & Crew: Ruff, Ruff, Repairs is an interactive literacy game that takes place in the Paw Patrol/Rubble and Crew universe. Players join Rubble and his crew to help repair various buildings across Builder's Cove. Players complete literacy minigames to earn the parts and tools they need for the repairs. As the developer on the this project, I was responsible for designing and implementing the mechanics, implementing art, animation VO, SFX, VFX, and music.

For this game, I had to develop an IK controller system for our Phaser engine which allows players to manipulate parts of animated characters in real time. In a previous game I had attempted to do the same thing, but simply did not have the time to work out a proper solution. This project gave me the chance revisit a failed solution and learn from it. Turns out my original solution wasn't that far off. It just need a few adjustments and worked perfectly. The IK controller was actually the least challenging part of this project. The most challenging part was actually optimization. There are 9 areas in this game full of animations, SFX, VFX, and music, but it needed to run on a device with 2GB of ram. It took quite a few tries to get it right and it was incredibly frustrating to fail over and over again, but despite all that this is still one of my favorite games. Simply because I was able to learn so much from it.

Bubble Guppies: Dive into the MET

Bubble Guppies: Dive into the MET is a collaboration between Noggin and the MET museum. Players explore actual works of art on display in the MET and create their own artwork using stickers and a pallete inspired from the art they just saw. Players then have the chance to hang their own art on a gallery wall for the guppies to enjoy. As the developer on the this project, I was responsible for designing and implementing the mechanics, implementing art, animation VO, SFX, VFX, and music.

This was my first solo project as a professional developer, and I learned quite a bit during it. Right around beta, we realized that the game was broken on certain devices. Come to find out that the technology I was using for 95% of the main mechanics in the game was WebGL only and those devices did not support it at the time. I had to completely rebuild the main mechanics in about a week in raw Canvas to stay on track of the deadline. I was able to get it done in 3 days and it looked even better than before.

Blaze & the Monster Machines: Ready, Set, Repair

Blaze & the Monster Machines: Ready, Set, Repair is an interactive literacy game that takes place in the Blaze universe. Players help repair various monster machines by completing literacy minigames to earn parts and tools for the repair. As the developer on the this project, I was responsible for designing and implementing the mechanics, implementing art, animation VO, SFX, VFX, and music.

This project is up there in terms of my favorites. The final product turned out really great, very snappy and fun. The only major hiccup in this project came with the vacuum hose. We didn't have an IK control system setup in engine yet, so I had to find a way around that. The solution came in the form of a bezier curve. I created a bezier curve in the shape of the vacuum hose and then drew overlapping points in the same color as the hose asset. From there it was just a matter of moving the head and updating the points. We did have to simplify the original asset, but this exact issue lead to the development of an in engine IK control system down the line.
